## LinkedIn anchor post

The first 10 customers of a B2B AI startup are not bought with marketing.

They are recruited.

Most B2B GTM advice assumes three things:

1. The product is deterministic.
2. The buyer knows what they want.
3. The demo shows the real product.

AI startups usually have none of these.

The product works beautifully on Tuesday and breaks on a weird input on Thursday.

The buyer cannot fully describe what "good output" means yet.

And your biggest competitor is not another startup. It is the customer's internal team trying to duct-tape the same workflow together.

So the first 10 customers need a different motion:

Pick a workflow you can run live in a meeting.

Trade integration work for design-partner rights.

Charge real money on day one, but keep it psychologically cheap.

Keep one founder across the whole loop: cold note, demo, integration, onboarding, 30-day check-in.

The uncomfortable truth:

Your first customers are not paying for software.

They are paying for founder attention.

The startup question is whether you can turn that attention into repeatable product learning instead of custom consulting.

That is the line.

If customer #1 becomes a custom branch forever, you built a services business.

If customer #1 teaches you how to sell customer #11 without rebuilding the product, you might have a startup.

I wrote the full playbook here.

## Standalone short post 1

Your first AI customers are not buying software.

They are buying founder attention.

That is fine.

The danger is pretending otherwise.

The real question:

Can you turn that attention into repeatable product learning, or are you just building custom consulting with a nicer landing page?

## Standalone short post 2

Free pilots are a trap for early B2B AI startups.

Free customers do not behave like real customers.

They avoid hard feedback.
They do not reveal budget pressure.
They do not churn honestly.

Charge something small.

The amount matters less than the fact that the customer made a real decision.

## Standalone short post 3

Before selling your B2B AI product, ask:

Can I run the workflow live in the sales call?

If not, the wedge is probably too abstract.

"We help analysts" is not a wedge.

"We turn this exact input into the output your team signs off on" is a wedge.

Specificity closes.
