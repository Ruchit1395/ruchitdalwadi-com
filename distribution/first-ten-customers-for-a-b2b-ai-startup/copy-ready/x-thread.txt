The first 10 customers of a B2B AI startup are not bought with marketing.

They are recruited.

Most teams get this wrong, then wonder why "GTM" feels impossible.

Here is the early playbook:

---

Normal B2B SaaS assumes:

- the product is deterministic
- the buyer knows the workflow
- the demo represents reality

AI startups often have none of these.

The product is still learning. So is the buyer.

---

Move 1:

Pick a workflow you can run live in a meeting.

"We help analysts" is too vague.

"We turn a 40-page legal brief into a 1-page summary your associate would sign" is runnable.

If you cannot demo it live, the wedge is too abstract.

---

Move 2:

Trade integration work for design-partner rights.

The unfair offer:

"We will build the integration into your stack in two weeks. In exchange, you give us weekly feedback for 90 days and permission to generalize what we learn."

---

Move 3:

Charge real money on day one.

Free customers do not behave like real customers.

They do not push back.
They do not churn.
They do not reveal what they actually value.

$500/month teaches more than a free pilot.

---

Move 4:

One founder owns the whole loop:

cold note -> demo -> integration -> onboarding -> 30-day check-in

Do not hire sales yet.

You are still learning the shape of the sale.

Outsourcing it too early destroys the learning.

---

The uncomfortable truth:

Your first 10 customers do not pay for software.

They pay for founder attention.

The startup question is whether that attention becomes repeatable product learning.
