# 48-hour engagement plan

## Objective

Earn distribution before asking for clicks.

For this campaign, comments are not "engagement chores." They are micro-posts placed under already-active conversations.

## Day -1: warm the topic

LinkedIn:

- Find 20 recent posts about AI startups, first customers, founder-led sales, design partners, AI GTM, or B2B SaaS.
- Leave 10 high-effort comments.
- Save 10 posts for day 0 and day +1 follow-up.

X:

- Find 20 active posts from founders/operators/investors discussing AI startups or early GTM.
- Reply to 15 with useful, non-promotional comments.
- Do not link the essay yet.

## Day 0: launch

T minus 60 minutes:

- Leave 5 LinkedIn comments on relevant posts.
- Leave 10 X replies on relevant posts.

Launch:

- Publish LinkedIn anchor post.
- Publish X thread.
- Add canonical link as first comment/reply.

First 60 minutes:

- Reply to every meaningful comment.
- Ask one follow-up question per serious commenter.
- If someone agrees, add a sharper example.
- If someone disagrees, thank them and ask where the framework breaks.

Hours 2-6:

- Comment on 10 more relevant LinkedIn posts.
- Reply to 10 more X posts.
- Send first 10 direct notes.

## Day +1

- Publish standalone short post 1 on X.
- If LinkedIn anchor slowed, post standalone short post 2 on LinkedIn.
- Send second batch of 10 direct notes.
- Turn the best reply/comment into a new post draft.

## Day +2

- Publish standalone short post 2 on X.
- Send final 10 direct notes.
- Comment on 20 relevant posts.
- Capture metrics.

## Comment frameworks

Use one of these, never generic praise.

### Add a missing distinction

Agree with the direction. The distinction I would add: the first AI customer is not just a customer. They are also part of the product discovery system. If the team does not negotiate the right to generalize the learning, the deal quietly becomes consulting.

### Add a concrete test

The live-demo test is underrated here. If the founder cannot run the workflow in the sales call on realistic customer input, the wedge is probably too abstract. The demo should teach both sides whether the problem is real.

### Productize the objection

One pattern I have seen: the objection that blocks the sale is often the next product feature. If every prospect asks "can this work inside our stack?", integration is not post-sale work. It is the product.

### Price/free-pilot angle

Free pilots create polite feedback. Small paid pilots create honest feedback. The price does not need to be huge, but the customer needs to make a real decision.

### Founder-led sales angle

The reason founder-led sales matters early is not charisma. It is compression. One person hears the objection, sees the workflow, owns the integration, and updates the product instinct in the same week.
