# Follow-up copy bank

Use these after the launch post/thread. They are designed to keep the same idea alive for 30 days without repeating the same post.

## LinkedIn follow-ups

### 1. Paid pilots

Free pilots are a trap for early B2B AI startups.

Not because revenue matters that much at customer #1.

Because truth matters.

A free pilot gives you polite feedback:

"This is interesting."
"We can see the potential."
"Circle back next quarter."

A small paid pilot gives you different feedback:

"This has to work by Friday."
"It needs to fit this workflow."
"This is the output my team would actually use."

The amount can be small.

The decision cannot be fake.

That is the real value of charging early.

You are not proving willingness to pay at scale yet.

You are proving that the pain is real enough for the customer to make a decision.

### 2. Consulting boundary

The line between a design partner and a consulting client is not the invoice.

It is the right to generalize the learning.

If customer #1 teaches you something that makes customer #11 easier to sell, onboard, and support, you are building a startup.

If customer #1 becomes a custom branch that only works for them, you are building services.

Both can be good businesses.

But they are not the same business.

The dangerous middle is calling it a design partnership while quietly accepting consulting constraints.

### 3. Founder-led sales

Founder-led sales is not about charisma.

It is about compression.

In one week, the founder can:

- hear the objection,
- run the demo,
- watch the workflow break,
- build the integration,
- onboard the user,
- and update the product instinct.

That loop is the company learning.

Hire sales too early and the learning gets filtered.

The founder gets notes instead of scars.

Notes are useful later.

Scars are useful now.

### 4. Customer 11

You are not ready for customer #11 just because customers #1-10 paid.

You are ready when:

1. You can predict who will sign before the demo.
2. The integration takes days, not weeks.
3. The customer's success metric is legible.
4. You know who you should not sell to.

Until then, the answer may be uncomfortable:

Hand-build customer #11 too.

The first ten are not a trophy.

They are a lab.

### 5. Live workflow

Before selling a B2B AI product, I would ask one question:

Can the founder run the workflow live in the sales call?

Not a deck.

Not a polished demo.

The actual workflow, on realistic customer input.

If the answer is no, the wedge is probably still too abstract.

"We help analysts" is not a wedge.

"Send me one brief and I will turn it into the summary your associate would sign" is a wedge.

Specificity closes.
