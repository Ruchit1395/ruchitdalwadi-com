Context engineering, minus the mystique: the six-slot inventory. Fill all six before blaming the model.

1. Goal. What does done look like, in one sentence? Failure mode: a paragraph of vibes where a sentence should be. If done takes three sentences, it is three tasks.

2. Constraints. What must never change without asking? Failure mode: constraints living in someone's head and surfacing only in the rejection comment.

3. Examples. One output you would ship and one you would reject. The pair teaches more than either alone. Failure mode: three shipped examples and zero rejects, so the model learns the style but never the line.

4. Sources. Which docs, tickets, or data does it read, and are they current? Failure mode: the stale policy. The model cites it perfectly and is confidently wrong.

5. Allowed actions. What can it do versus only suggest? Failure mode: discovering this boundary after the action, in an incident channel.

6. Review rule. What does the checker look for in 30 seconds? Failure mode: "looks good to me", which means there is no rule.

When an output disappoints, walk the six slots and find the empty one. In my experience it is almost always slot 3 or slot 4. The instructions were fine; the model was working from a blank desk. Fill the slots once and next week's task starts at 80 percent instead of zero.
