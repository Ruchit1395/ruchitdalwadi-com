You can settle a model debate in an afternoon if you run the steps in the right order.

The order is the whole trick. The afternoon bake-off, with the failure mode at each step:

Step 1. Write the expensive mistake first. Not "accuracy": the specific failure that costs money or trust. Refunding an ambiguous case. Inventing a citation. Deleting the wrong rows. Failure mode: writing a category instead of a mistake. "Bad output" cannot be tested. "Promises a delivery date we cannot meet" can.

Step 2. Build 20 cases around that mistake: 10 where the right move is to act, 10 where the right move is to hesitate, ask, or decline. Failure mode: inventing all 20 from imagination. Pull at least half from the real queue, ugly formatting included.

Step 3. Run every candidate on all 20. Log acts-when-it-should-not and hesitates-when-it-should-not as separate columns. Failure mode: averaging them into one score. They are different costs, and in most products one of them is ten times the other.

Step 4. Only now look at price and latency, and only among the survivors. Failure mode: letting a 40 percent cost saving argue a failed candidate back into the running. It already failed on the mistake you said you could not afford.

Most model debates are step 4 arguments between people who skipped steps 1 through 3. The leaderboard cannot know which mistake is expensive for you. That part was always your job.
