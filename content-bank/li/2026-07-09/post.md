The leaderboard can tell you which model writes the smartest average answer. It cannot tell you which mistake would be expensive for your business, and that is the deciding variable.

For a support workflow, the expensive mistake might be refunding an ambiguous case. For research, a confident invented citation. For a coding agent, editing a file nobody asked it to touch. Different mistakes, different winners. Sometimes the "weaker" model wins because it hesitates in exactly the right places.

I ran this exercise on an ed-tech grading feature: twenty test cases, ten where the right move was to act and ten where the right move was to escalate to a teacher. The benchmark favorite acted on all twenty. The runner-up escalated eight of the ten it should have. The runner-up shipped.

Write the eval in business language, not benchmark language. Twenty test cases built around your costliest failure beat any leaderboard screenshot.
